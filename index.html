In interacting with this class, I’ve realized one key thing: once you add any element of interactivity to a piece of theatre, it stops being just a show… and turns into a game. Once the audience has any interactivity, they become capable of failing to do what you want them to, and in turn, the likelihood that your piece of theatre succeeds becomes uncertain. Because of this, it’s vital to set up your piece so that you minimize the risk of derailing, so that every audience member gets the maximum enjoyment out of it.



There are many ways to design a game, narratively speaking. The “Linear” or “Railroad” system is as simple as a show, or a movie. A perfectly linear game has no interactivity in the slightest, but the vast majority of games have at least some interaction. While most theatre pieces will conclude whether the audience participates or not, this piece requires the audience members to interact with it in order to conclude in a satisfying way. Picture it like a system of levels and checkpoints. In the world of, say, Halo, you need to reach a certain point on the map for the level to progress. However, by rushing straight to that point you can skip huge chunks of the intended route accidentally. If a developer gives the audience access to that route, it is up to that developer to guide the story in a way that avoids the shortcut being the main direction people accidentally go.



I see many of our immersive theatre pieces through this lens, and in many cases, it disappoints me. So many immersive experiences were as simple as a person having us sit on the floor and taste or touch or hear something, and then calling it a day. The biggest piece missing was the drive to keep going. The wonder as to what’s around the bend, or the excitement for what just happened letting us coast along to see what comes next. In many cases I found myself trying to “game the system”. To take a path that wasn’t anticipated, or to take notice when I missed a cue or left behind an interaction. Which in a roundabout way was the lesson the class was trying to teach us, so I suppose it succeeded? If everything always went perfectly to plan, we would never learn anything. Still, there is a direct link between effort and results in work like this, and it always shows through.

There’s a riddle I’ve heard about Apollo, the God of Theatre. It says when the sun sets, it sets north of Central Park. That or the Netherlands, occasionally. If only I could find where Apollo rests, maybe I’d get to speak to him.

The original movie Saw was criticized in a later installment of the work for being an unwinnable game. At the outset of the puzzle, the main character unconsciously took an action that led to their own inevitable death, making the death game no longer a game, but simply a murder. Only a player smart enough to notice the mistake without ever knowing why it was a mistake would be able to find this piece of the puzzle, which is one of the most unfair possible strategies to game design, but equally one of the most rewarding to a player who completes it successfully.


You didn’t need to do this one. You didn’t need to do any of them, really. The solution was right there. Whether you chose to do it or not, an aspect of the game is not forcing a player to take exactly the route you planned them to, but to present them with an obstacle and permit them to find their own solution, even if that solution may be cheesing their way out of putting in the work. The next password may be hiding right underneath your nose. Behind the cover art lies a tool you will need. On the 25th page of Carol Oja’s academia, you will create the 10th password.


Some puzzles are ones you can see, but don’t have the tools to solve until much later. It contributes to the overall expansiveness of the game, to see your answer but not yet have the keys to solve it. Sometimes the trick is hidden right under your nose. In fact, if you collect every password you’ve found in this game, you may find they have a common thread. The first letter of each is the final password of the project.


The process of building a game, especially a puzzle game, is to begin with the ending. By now, you’ve probably gone through a whole lot of challenge for not a whole lot of reward. As Suits said, though, the purpose of a game is to voluntarily attempt to overcome unnecessary obstacles. You’ve taken a ride through your own curriculum, and seen just how each aspect of the subject can challenge your perception of the world. Not for the simple experience of being told you’re being immersed, not for a two-minute taste party, but for a higher purpose. An effect. You can’t force people to go through a game if they don’t want to, but if they’re willing to put in the effort, you can make damn sure it’s worth it.
